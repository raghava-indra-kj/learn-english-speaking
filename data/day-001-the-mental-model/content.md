# Day 1 — The Mental Model

*By the end of today you will see the hidden skeleton under every English sentence. Once you can see it, you stop building sentences word by word — and you stop freezing in the middle of them.*

## What you'll be able to do

- I can find the three core parts of any sentence — the subject, the verb, and the object.
- I can build a correct sentence from one of just five simple shapes, instead of guessing.
- I can tell when I am "writing an essay out loud," and switch to short, natural spoken English.
- I know which stage of fluency I am at, so I practise the one thing that will help me most right now.

## Why this matters

Picture this. In a meeting, your manager turns to you and asks what happened with the client report. You know the answer perfectly — the whole story is clear in your head. But when you open your mouth, the English comes out slowly. You are quietly translating from your first language, and your first language lines words up in a different order than English does. So the sentence arrives scrambled, or with long pauses in the middle, and you end up sounding far less sure than you actually are.

That gap — between what you know and what you can say — is almost never a knowledge problem. It is a *structure* problem. Today we fix the structure. We are not doing grammar drills; we are installing the frame that your brain will reach for automatically, so the words fall into the right order without you having to think about each one. Think of it as setting up the foundation before you build the rooms. Every other day in this course sits on top of what you put in place now.

## The words you need

Before we start, here are the few terms we'll use today. You may have met them in school and forgotten them — that is completely normal, and you don't need them memorised, only understood. Read each one slowly.

A **noun** is a naming word: a person, a thing, a place, or an idea. *Manager, report, office, deadline, decision* are all nouns. If you can put "the" in front of it and it still makes sense — *the manager, the deadline* — it is almost certainly a noun.

A **verb** is the action or "being" word. It is the engine of the sentence; nothing happens without it. *Send, approve, finish, is, was* are all verbs. To find the verb, ask "what is happening here?"

A **subject** is the person or thing the sentence is *about* — the one doing the action, or the one being described. It is the starting point of the sentence. In "The manager approved the budget," the subject is *the manager*, because she is the one doing the approving. The simplest way to find the subject is to find the verb first, then ask "who or what is doing this?" The answer is your subject. (This single habit — find the verb, then ask who is behind it — will rescue you again and again, so it is worth saying out loud once now.)

An **object** is the person or thing that *receives* the action — the thing the verb is done *to*. In "I sent the report," the action is *sent*, and the thing that got sent is *the report*, so *the report* is the object. To find it, say the subject and verb, then ask "what?" or "whom?": *I sent… what? The report.*

An **adjective** is a word that describes a noun: a *tight* deadline, a *clear* summary, a *difficult* client. It adds colour to a naming word.

An **adverb** is a word that describes a verb — it tells you *how* something is done: she replied *quickly*, he explained it *clearly*. A useful clue: many (not all) adverbs end in *-ly*.

A **clause** is simply a group of words that has its own subject and its own verb. "The deadline moved" is a clause. Most of what we do this month is learning to build clauses and join them together neatly.

That's the whole vocabulary for today. Keep these meanings nearby; we'll lean on them constantly.

## The lesson

### English runs Subject → Verb → Object — and the order is not optional

Almost every English sentence flows in one direction: first the **subject**, then the **verb**, then the **object**. "I (subject) sent (verb) the report (object)." "The client (subject) approved (verb) the proposal (object)." Say a few of these and you can feel the rhythm — the doer, the action, the thing it lands on.

Here is the part that surprises many learners: in English you usually *cannot* move those pieces around. The position of a word is what tells you its job. "The manager emailed the client" and "The client emailed the manager" use the exact same words, but they mean opposite things, purely because the words sit in different seats. Whoever sits in the subject seat did the emailing; whoever sits in the object seat received it.

Why is English so strict about this, when your own language may let you rearrange words freely? Because long ago English lost most of its word-endings — the little tails that some languages add to a word to mark whether it is the doer or the receiver. Having thrown those tails away, English had to find another way to show who did what to whom, and the way it chose was *position*. So word order in English is not a style choice; it is doing the same job that word-endings do in other languages. That is also why, under pressure, a habit from your first language can leak out — many Indian and regional languages place the verb at the end, so a tired brain produces "I the report sent." The fix is to trust the frame: doer, then action, then the thing. Keep the verb in the middle, where English keeps it.

*Try this:* put these words in the correct order — [ the email / I / sent ].
Answer: "I sent the email." Subject (*I*) → verb (*sent*) → object (*the email*).

### Every sentence is one of just five shapes

The blank-page fear — that moment where you don't know how to begin — comes from feeling that a sentence could be anything. It can't. Almost every English sentence is one of only five shapes, and once you can see the five, you stop searching for words at random and simply pick a shape and pour your words into its slots.

The first shape is **subject + verb** on its own: "The meeting started." Nothing receives the action; the sentence is complete with just a doer and an action. The second adds an object — **subject + verb + object**: "I sent the report." This is the workhorse of office English. The third has *two* objects, because some verbs hand one thing to someone — **subject + verb + two objects**: "I sent my manager the report" (I sent *the report* to *my manager*). The fourth shape uses a being-verb to *describe* the subject rather than act on something — **subject + verb + a describing word**: "The deadline is tight." Here *tight* doesn't receive an action; it tells you what the deadline *is like*. The fifth and last shape describes the *object* instead of the subject — **subject + verb + object + a describing word**: "We call this process onboarding." The word *onboarding* renames or describes *this process*.

You do not need to memorise the labels. You need to recognise that there are only five frames, and that the difference between them is small: how many things follow the verb, and whether what follows *receives* the action or *describes* something. When you know it is only ever one of five, beginning a sentence stops feeling like jumping off a cliff.

One trap lives inside the fourth shape. The describing word after a being-verb describes the *subject*, so it must be an adjective, not an adverb. We say "The deadline is tight," not "The deadline is tightly," because we are describing the deadline itself (a noun), and adjectives are what describe nouns.

*Try this:* which shape is "The proposal looks strong"?
Answer: subject + verb + describing word (the fourth shape). *Strong* describes the subject *the proposal*, so it is an adjective doing its job correctly.

### Words have jobs, not just labels

It is tempting to learn grammar as a set of labels to stick on words. A far more useful habit is to ask what *job* a word is doing in this particular sentence. The same word can do different jobs in different places, so the job is what really matters. Nouns name the things, verbs move the sentence forward, and adjectives and adverbs add colour — an adjective colouring a noun, an adverb colouring a verb.

Why is this the more powerful way to think? Because when a sentence sounds wrong but you can't say why, you can diagnose it with a single question: "What job is this word doing here, and is it the right *kind* of word for that job?" That one question quietly fixes most of the errors you'll meet for the rest of this course. Take "She replied quick." The word *quick* is trying to tell us *how* she replied — that is colouring the verb *replied*. But colouring a verb is an adverb's job, and *quick* is an adjective. Swap in the adverb and it clicks: "She replied quickly."

*Try this:* fix this sentence — "The system processes requests slow."
Answer: "The system processes requests **slowly**." *Slowly* is colouring the verb *processes* (it tells us how), and colouring a verb is the adverb's job.

### Spoken English is short chunks, not written essays

Many learners are taught a heavy, formal written style and then try to *speak* it. The result sounds robotic, and worse, it slows you down — every extra word is one more thing to assemble while people wait. Good spoken English is short and direct. It moves in small idea-chunks, one at a time, because that is how a listener actually takes it in.

Compare "I would like to inform you that the task has been completed" with "The task is done." They carry the same meaning, but the first makes the listener wade through seven words of packaging before the point arrives, while the second lands immediately. The long formal openers — *I would like to inform you that…, With reference to your earlier email…, As per our discussion…* — add politeness theatre but no meaning, and they are exactly the phrases that make non-native speech sound stiff. In speech, drop the wrapper and state the point. You will sound more confident, not less, because confident people get to the point.

This is not about being blunt or rude; it is about respecting the listener's time and reducing your own load. You can still be warm — "Thanks, that's done" is short *and* friendly.

*Try this:* make this sound like natural speech — "I would like to update you that the report has been completed by me."
Answer: "I've finished the report." (Or simply "The report's done.")

### Know your fluency stage, so you train the right thing

Fluency does not arrive all at once; it comes in four stages, and they build in order. The first is **accuracy** — getting the grammar basically right. The second is **vocabulary** — having the precise words ready. The third is **chunking** — thinking in whole phrases instead of single words, which is what removes those mid-sentence pauses. The fourth is **speed** — producing it automatically, under pressure, without planning.

Knowing the order saves you from a common, demoralising mistake: chasing speed too early. If you try to talk fast before your grammar is steady, you simply become fast at making errors, and the errors set like concrete. Build the floor first. Right now, in week one, your job is accuracy — and that means slow, careful speech is not a weakness, it is exactly the stage doing its work. The speed comes last, on purpose, once everything underneath it is solid.

*Try this:* you mostly get your grammar right, but you pause a lot because you're picking words one at a time in your head. Which stage should you focus on next?
Answer: chunking (stage three). Your accuracy is in place; the pauses come from single-word retrieval, and chunking is the habit that replaces it.

## See it work together

Let's watch the frame do real work on three everyday moments.

First, turning a messy thought into clean speech. Suppose your head is holding: *"report, the client one, I finished it, it's with the manager now."* Don't try to say it all in one breath. Pick a shape for each piece. "I finished the client report" is subject + verb + object. "It's with the manager now" is subject + verb + describing detail. Two short, correct chunks beat one long scramble every time, and your listener follows easily.

Second, repairing a sentence on the fly. A colleague says, "I am informing that the system responds slow." You can fix it with today's tools alone: make it spoken English by dropping the formal opener, and fix the word-job so the adverb colours the verb. It becomes "The system responds **slowly**," and if you want to add ownership, "and I'm looking into why."

Third, spotting the shape live. Someone says, "We call this stage approval." Run the frame: *we* is the subject, *call* is the verb, *this stage* is the object, and *approval* is a word describing that object. That's the fifth shape — and recognising it means you could build your own version instantly: "They call this step onboarding."

## Practice (do this — don't just read)

Reading these explanations is not what makes them stick; producing your own sentences is. Work through all four parts, out loud where you can.

**A. Warm-up — label and name.** For each sentence, point to the subject, the verb, and the object (if there is one), and name which of the five shapes it is.
1. The meeting started.
2. I approved the invoice.
3. The team sent the client an update.
4. The deadline looks tight.
5. We named the project "Horizon."

**B. Build it — from your own work.** Write one sentence in each of the five shapes, using your real job. Keep them true and simple.

**C. Say it out loud — your Day 1 baseline (record this).** Record yourself for two minutes on your phone, answering: *"What is your job? What are you working on right now? What is blocking you?"* This is your "before" snapshot — do not edit it or re-record it to sound good. Save the file. On Day 30 you will record the same thing and hear, in your own voice, how far you've come.

**D. Explain it back.** In two or three sentences, in your own words, explain to an imaginary colleague *why* English keeps a strict word order. If you can teach it simply, you own it.

> **Answer key and model answers**
>
> **A.** (1) Subject: *the meeting*; verb: *started* — shape: subject + verb. (2) Subject: *I*; verb: *approved*; object: *the invoice* — subject + verb + object. (3) Subject: *the team*; verb: *sent*; objects: *the client* (to whom) + *an update* (what) — subject + verb + two objects. (4) Subject: *the deadline*; verb: *looks*; describing word: *tight* — subject + verb + describing word. (5) Subject: *we*; verb: *named*; object: *the project*; describing word: *"Horizon"* — subject + verb + object + describing word.
>
> **B. (model)** Subject + verb: "The call ended." Subject + verb + object: "I reviewed the contract." Subject + verb + two objects: "I sent the vendor a reminder." Subject + verb + describing word: "The plan is ready." Subject + verb + object + describing word: "We consider this account critical."
>
> **C. (what to do after recording)** Listen back once, only for these three things: word-order slips (the verb landing in the wrong place), long formal openers ("I would like to inform you…"), and any mistake you make more than once. Write down your **top three errors** and date them. Don't try to fix them today — just name them. That short list is your personal map for the next 29 days.
>
> **D. (model)** "English keeps a strict order because it dropped the word-endings that some languages use to show who did what. With those endings gone, English uses *position* instead — the subject comes first, the verb next, the object last — so moving the words changes the meaning. That's why 'the manager emailed the client' and 'the client emailed the manager' are not the same."

## Common mistakes to never make again

- ✗ "I the report sent." → ✓ "I sent the report." The verb belongs in the middle, not at the end — keep the doer, action, thing order.
- ✗ "She replied quick." → ✓ "She replied quickly." An adverb colours the verb; *quick* is an adjective.
- ✗ "The deadline is tightly." → ✓ "The deadline is tight." After a being-verb you describe the subject, so use the adjective.
- ✗ "I would like to inform you that the task has been completed." → ✓ "The task is done." Drop the formal wrapper and state the point.
- ✗ "I am having a doubt." → ✓ "I have a question." (A preview of Day 3 — *have* here is a state, so no *-ing*.)

## Quick summary (60-second cheat sheet)

- English runs **Subject → Verb → Object**, and the order is fixed because position carries the meaning. Keep the verb in the middle.
- Every sentence is one of **five shapes**: subject+verb; +object; +two objects; +a word describing the subject; +a word describing the object.
- Words have **jobs**. When something sounds off, ask: "What job is this word doing, and is it the right kind of word for it?" (Adjectives colour nouns; adverbs colour verbs.)
- Speak in **short chunks**. Cut formal openers like "I would like to inform you that…" and state the point.
- Fluency builds in order: **accuracy → vocabulary → chunking → speed.** You are on accuracy now, so slow and correct is exactly right.
